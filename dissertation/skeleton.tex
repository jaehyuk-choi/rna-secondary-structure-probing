\abstract{
RNA foundation models (RNA FMs) pretrained on large sequence corpora are increasingly adopted as representation backbones for RNA secondary structure prediction, often achieving strong results when paired with task-specific prediction heads. As these pipelines become more widespread, a natural question is whether the pretrained representation itself already encodes base-pairing information or whether that information is learned primarily by the prediction head during supervised training. We investigate this by training low-rank bilinear probes on fixed embeddings from five encoder-only models and decoding pairwise base-pairing scores under varying allowed pairing constraints. Recoverability of base-pairing information varies sharply across models. ERNIE-RNA reaches F1 of 0.51 on an in-distribution test set and 0.46 on unseen RNA families, while most other RNA encoders remain below 0.14 under the same protocol. RoBERTa, despite lacking any RNA-specific pretraining, outperforms several RNA-trained encoders, and its advantage grows once allowed pairing constraints compensate for the chemical specificity its representations lack. This shows that measured performance reflects not only what a representation encodes but also how the evaluation is set up.
 
To test whether recovered signals have practical value, we inject probe-derived scores as soft priors into CPLfold. Under CONTRAfold, every encoder benefits, with ERNIE-RNA gaining up to 21.8\% relative F1 over the unaugmented baseline. Under ViennaRNA, gains are largely confined to ERNIE-RNA and can degrade under distribution shift, suggesting that the usefulness of probe-derived priors depends on the folding backend. Together, these results provide controlled evidence that some, but not all, RNA foundation models encode recoverable and practically useful base-pairing information in their pretrained representations.
}

\section{Motivation \& Context}\label{sec:intro_motivation}

Ribonucleic acid (RNA) molecules serve diverse regulatory and catalytic roles 
that extend well beyond their classical function as intermediaries of genetic 
information \citep{alonso2021mechanisms}. In many cases, RNA function depends on the formation of specific folded structures \citep{tinoco1999how,ganser2019roles}. 
% These structures arise from base-pairing interactions between nucleotides (A, U, G, C), most commonly the canonical Watson–Crick pairs (AU and GC), which are energetically more stable and therefore more likely to form during folding. 
These structures arise from base-pairing interactions between nucleotides (A, U, G, and C). The most common are the canonical Watson–Crick pairs, adenine–uracil (A–U) and guanine–cytosine (G–C), which are typically more thermodynamically stable and therefore more likely to form during RNA folding \citep{sponer2005principles}.
Such structural elements create binding interfaces for interactions with proteins and other RNAs. Beyond this structural role, RNA molecules can adopt multiple conformations, enabling dynamic regulation of these interactions \citep{tants2024rna}. 
The recent success of mRNA-based therapeutics, most prominently COVID-19 vaccines, has further underscored the practical importance of understanding RNA structural properties \citep{chaudhary2021mrna, moschioni2026beyond, li2026clinical}. Since RNA sequence influences folding, sequence-level design choices can directly affect stability and translational efficiency through their structural consequences \citep{chaudhary2021mrna, jin2025mrna, zhou2026mrna}.

Among the different levels of RNA structure, secondary structure, defined by patterns of intramolecular base pairing, offers a particularly useful level of analysis \citep{vandivier2016conservation, bai2016atlas}. It remains simple enough to model directly, while still capturing important
determinants of stability and molecular recognition. Accordingly, predicting RNA
secondary structure from sequence has long been a central problem in RNA
bioinformatics \citep{mathews2019benchmark}. The field has been shaped by classical
thermodynamic frameworks such as ViennaRNA \citep{lorenz2011viennarna} as well as
learning-based approaches ranging from early probabilistic models such as CONTRAfold
\citep{do2006contrafold} to more recent deep learning methods including E2Efold,
UFold, and MXfold2 \citep{chen2020e2efold,fu2022ufold,sato2021mxfold2}.

At the same time, the rapid development of large language models (LLMs) has encouraged
the adaptation of language-modelling ideas to biological sequences
\citep{sarumi2024llmbioinformatics}. In this line of work, DNA, RNA, and protein
sequences are treated as discrete sequences over finite alphabets. As a result, models can be pretrained on large unlabelled corpora to learn contextual representations that are reusable across downstream tasks. Early and influential examples include
DNABERT for genomic DNA \citep{ji2021dnabert} and protein language models trained
on hundreds of millions of sequences \citep{elnaggar2022prottrans,rives2021biological}.
Within this broader trend, RNA foundation models \textbf{(RNA FMs)} have emerged as a rapidly developing area, with encoder-based architectures pretrained on large RNA corpora to produce nucleotide-level representations for a range of downstream tasks \citep{chen2022rnafm,yin2025ernierna,penic2025rinalmo}. These tasks include secondary-structure prediction, RNA contact-map prediction or RNA–protein binding prediction \citep{tahmid2025birna, singh2019rna}.

This raises a central question for RNA foundation models. It remains unclear whether pretrained representations encode RNA secondary structure, particularly base-pairing patterns. Alternatively, Alternatively, they may mainly provide inputs to task-specific heads, which learn structural predictions during supervised training on labelled data. This distinction is
important because strong downstream performance alone does not demonstrate that
secondary-structure information is already present in the pretrained representations.
Observed gains may instead reflect the capacity of the prediction head or reliance on
features that correlate with structure without directly representing base-pairing patterns
\citep{hewitt2019control}. Answering this question therefore requires a controlled
representation-level analysis that is separate from end-to-end downstream performance.

This study uses \textbf{structural probing} as a controlled method to study this question \citep{hewitt2019structural}. Since RNA secondary structure is fundamentally defined by base-pairing interactions, we ask \textbf{whether base-pairing information is already encoded in the outputs of pretrained RNA foundation models}. To test this, simple probes are trained to predict pairwise base-pairing scores from model outputs, allowing us to examine whether such information can be recovered and how this varies across layers \citep{hewitt2019structural,belinkov2022probing}. We then ask a second, more practical question, asking \textbf{whether the same probe-derived scores improve RNA structure prediction when incorporated as additional pairwise evidence within a structured folding framework}. This two-stage design separates the
question of what pretrained representations encode from the question of
whether the encoded information is useful in practice.

\section{Approach overview}
\label{sec:intro_overview}
Our analysis proceeds in two stages.

In the first stage, we extract token-level embeddings from each RNA foundation model layer, keeping encoder weights fixed. We then train a low-rank bilinear probe \citep{hewitt2019structural} to produce a pairwise score matrix over nucleotide pairs, interpreted as a contact map of base-pairing preferences. Since RNA contact maps are extremely sparse, positive pairs constitute roughly 0.2\% of all candidate pairs, we employ balanced pair sampling during training. Evaluation is performed under three allowed pairing constraints: 
\textbf{unconstrained} (all pair types permitted),
\textbf{canonical} (Watson--Crick pairs only, A--U and G--C),
and \textbf{canonical-wobble} (Watson--Crick pairs plus G--U
wobble pairs). G--U wobble pairs are non-canonical but are included in the canonical-wobble setting because they occur frequently in RNA structures \citep{varani2000wobble}.
% unconstrained, Watson-Crick only, and Watson-Crick plus GU wobble. 
Predicted structures are obtained via greedy max-one decoding with a threshold selected exclusively on the validation split. All settings (layer, probe rank $k$, decoding mode, threshold $\tau$) are selected on the validation set and fixed before held-out evaluation on both an in-distribution test set (TS0) and a family-shifted out-of-distribution set (NEW).

In the second stage, we inject probe-derived pairwise scores as soft bonuses into CPLfold \citep{wang2026cplfold}, a structured folding framework that augments its scoring objective with an $\alpha$-weighted prior term. We evaluate two folding backends: ViennaRNA \citep{lorenz2011viennarna}, which uses thermodynamic energy parameters, and CONTRAfold \citep{do2006contrafold}, which uses a learned scoring function. The prior weight $\alpha$ is swept on the validation split and fixed for all held-out evaluation. In this setup, the probe provides local pairwise scores derived from pretrained representations, estimating how likely each nucleotide pair is to form a base pair. The folding backend then enforces global structural constraints, such as allowing each nucleotide to participate in at most one base pair, to produce a valid secondary structure (Section~\ref{sec:methods_problem}).

Together, the two stages allow us to assess not only whether structural information is present in pretrained embeddings, but also whether it provides practical value when combined with physics-based or learned folding constraints. Detailed experimental procedures are described in Chapters~\ref{ch:methods} and~\ref{ch:experiments}.

\section{RNA Secondary Structure}
% Ver2.
Ribonucleic acid (RNA) is a single-stranded nucleic acid composed of four nucleotides, Adenine (A), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G), and Uracil (U), and it plays a central role in biological information processing \citep{alonso2021mechanisms}. Beyond its role in storing and transmitting genetic information, RNA can also carry out molecular functions directly through its structure. As a result, RNA function is shaped not only by sequence but also by the intramolecular base-pairing interactions that gives rise to RNA secondary structure \citep{ganser2019roles, tinoco1999how}.

\textbf{Levels of RNA structure.} RNA Structure is conventionally described at three levels \citep{tinoco1999how,turner2009nndb}: \textbf{Primary}, \textbf{Secondary},
and \textbf{Tertiary} structure. The primary structure is the linear nucleotide sequence
$x = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$, where each $x_i \in \Sigma = \{A,C,G,U\}$. 
Tertiary structure refers to the full three-dimensional geometry of the molecule,
but is experimentally difficult to obtain and computationally expensive to model.

Between these two levels lies RNA secondary structure, which captures the
major base-pairing interactions. A secondary structure can be
represented as a set of base pairs $P \subseteq \{(i,j) \mid 1 \le i < j \le n\}$, where $(i,j)\in\mathcal{P}$ indicates that nucleotides $i$ and $j$ form a
base pair.

\textbf{Base-pairing types.}
The most common base pairs are the canonical Watson--Crick pairs, A--U and G--C, which are thermodynamically more stable and account for the majority of naturally observed pairings \citep{sponer2005principles}. A third frequently observed pairing is the G--U wobble pair, which is weaker than canonical pairs but occurs widely in tRNA, rRNA, and other structured RNAs \citep{varani2000gu}. Non-canonical pairings beyond G--U also occur, particularly in tertiary interactions, but are not the focus of this thesis.
 
\textbf{Structural constraints.}
Not every possible pair set corresponds to a physically realisable structure. Constraints on the predicted pair set can be divided into \textbf{local} and \textbf{global} categories. Local constraints restrict which nucleotide pair types are admissible including canonical pairs. Global constraints determine whether a full set of predicted pairs forms a valid structure. One such constraint is the max-one pairing condition, which requires each nucleotide to participate in at most one base pair \citep{zuker1981optimal}. 
Some classical algorithms additionally assume pseudoknot-freeness, prohibiting crossing pairs of the form $(i,j)$ and $(k,\ell)$ with $i<k<j<\ell$, because
allowing pseudoknots makes exact inference NP-hard in the general case
\citep{LyngsoPedersen2000}. However, pseudoknots do occur in biological
RNA structures, and some recent methods, including CPLfold
\citep{wang2026cplfold}, relax this assumption.
% In many classical formulations, validity also requires the structure to be pseudoknot-free, excluding crossing pairs of the form $(i,j)$ and $(k,\ell)$ with $i<k<j<\ell$ \citep{LyngsoPedersen2000}. 
Section~\ref{sec:methods_problem} provides further details on how these constraints are handled.

\textbf{Representations.}
Secondary structure is often represented in dot-bracket notation, as an arc
diagram, or as a contact map $M\in\{0,1\}^{n\times n}$ where $M_{ij}=1$ if and
only if $(i,j)\in\mathcal{P}$ \citep{mathews2019benchmark}. In this dissertation, secondary structure prediction
is formulated as pairwise contact prediction followed by a decoding step that
enforces the relevant structural constraints.

RNA secondary structure captures the major intramolecular base-pairing interactions in a simpler and more computationally accessible form than full three-dimensional tertiary structure. Therefore, it has been the main focus of computational RNA structure prediction for more than four decades \citep{zuker1981optimal,tinoco1999how}.
Experimental methods for determining RNA structure remain difficult to apply at large scale, so computational prediction is still the main practical approach to study RNA structure at scale \citep{mathews2019benchmark}.

Predicted secondary structures inform a range of downstream tasks, from
modelling RNA--protein interactions and identifying regulatory elements
to guiding mRNA sequence design where folding stability directly affect translational efficiency
\citep{chaudhary2021mrna,jin2025mrna}. This broad range of applications has led to continued methodological development, which we review in three parts below: (i)~classical thermodynamic energy minimisation,
(ii)~supervised learning from annotated structures, and (iii)~recent
pipelines that use pretrained RNA encoders to generate representations for downstream prediction.

\subsection{Classical Thermodynamic Models}
\label{sec:bg_thermo}
Classical approaches are based on the principle that RNA tends to fold into thermodynamically stable structures \citep{tinoco1999how}, and accordingly formulate RNA secondary structure prediction as a free-energy minimisation problem over possible base-pairing configurations \citep{zuker1981optimal}. Under the nearest-neighbor thermodynamic model \cite{turner2009nndb}, each candidate base-pairing structure is assigned a free energy, and the predicted structure corresponds to the minimum free energy (MFE) configuration \cite{zuker1981optimal}. As implemented in ViennaRNA \cite{lorenz2011viennarna}, combining with dynamic programming algorithms enable polynomial-time inference for pseudoknot-free RNA secondary structures.

Moreover, extending inference to pseudoknots is computationally costly \cite{LyngsoPedersen2000}. More recently, structured decoding frameworks have been developed that incorporate external pairwise evidence into the folding objective. One such framework is CPLfold, which augments thermodynamic or learned scoring with soft constraints derived from experimental or computational sources and supports pseudoknot prediction for long RNAs \citep{wang2026cplfold}. Its use in this study is described in detail in the following chapters.

\subsection{Foundation Models for Sequence Representation Learning}

Foundation models for biological sequences are based on the pretraining framework first established in natural language processing (NLP). A large model is trained on unlabelled sequence data using a self-supervised objective and then reused, often without further weight updates, as a feature extractor for downstream tasks \citep{vaswani2017attention,devlin2019bert}. These models can be implemented in several architectures forms, including encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder transformers. Encoder-only models produce contextual embeddings at each input position and are therefore widely used in embedding-based downstream tasks, while decoder-only and encoder-decoder models
are more commonly used for generation and sequence transformation \citep{devlin2019bert,radford2019gpt2,raffel2020t5}.

BERT-style masked language models are a widely used encoder-only architecture in NLP \citep{devlin2019bert}. They are trained to reconstruct randomly masked tokens from bidirectional context, and in doing so learn per-token representations that can be reused without further training of the encoder.  RoBERTa \citep{liu2019roberta} is a widely used refinement of the BERT framework. We include RoBERTa in our study as a non-RNA baseline. By evaluating RoBERTa and RNA-specific encoders under the same setup, we test whether RNA pretraining provides a measurable advantage over a general-purpose encoder trained without RNA data.

RNA FMs adapt this pretraining strategy to RNA sequences, producing contextual nucleotide embeddings that can be reused across downstream tasks. Existing RNA sequence models span encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder architectures.

In this study, we focus on encoder-only models. As RNA secondary structure often involves long-range base-pairing interactions between distant nucleotides, bidirectional contextual embeddings are well suited to capturing these dependencies and to controlled layer-wise probing of structural signal.

\textbf{RNABERT} \citep{akiyama2022rnabert} is an early RNA-specific encoder that adapts BERT-style training to RNA

its smaller scale, it is historically notable as one of the first encoder-only models pretrained specifically on RNA.

\textbf{RNA-FM} \citep{chen2022rnafm} is a larger encoder pretrained on approximately 23~million non-coding RNA sequences, producing 640-dimensional embeddings across 12 transformer layers.
 
\textbf{ERNIE-RNA} \citep{yin2025ernierna} retains a 12-layer encoder architecture of similar scale to RNA-FM, while introducing mechanisms motivated by RNA structure. \section{Interpretability and Probing for Biological FMs}
\label{sec:bg_interpretability}
The preceding sections reviewed RNA secondary structure and modern prediction pipelines, including FM-backed approaches (Section~\ref{sec:bg_fm_backed}) that combine pretrained encoders with task-specific heads and constraint-based decoding. 
Strong downstream performance from such systems does not, on its own, reveal the source of the useful structural signal. It remains unclear whether this signal is already present in the pretrained representations or is introduced mainly by the downstream head or decoder. This motivates a representation-level interpretability perspective. More specifically, the key question is whether pretrained embeddings, before any task-specific training, already encode RNA structural information, particularly base-pairing patterns.
% Strong downstream performance from such systems does not, on its own, reveal whether the pretrained representations already contain useful structural information, or whether the gains mainly come from the downstream head or decoder. This motivates a representation-level interpretability perspective: testing whether pretrained embeddings, before any task-specific training, already encode RNA structural information, specifically base-pairing patterns.

\subsection{Representation-level interpretability}
In NLP, interpretability broadly refers to explaining model behavior in a way that helps humans understand why a model produces a particular output for a given input \citep{doshi2017towards,belinkov2022analysis}. This goes beyond comparing predictive accuracy and instead examines what information is represented inside the model and how it is transformed across layers \citep{rogers2020primer,belinkov2022probing}. Interpretability analysis is commonly motivated by reliability and robustness assessment, and evaluation of ethical risks such as bias \citep{ribeiro2016trust,sundararajan2017axiomatic,barocas2016big}. Prior surveys distinguish several levels of analysis, including input-level (which input features matter), representation-level (what information is encoded in hidden states), and mechanism-level (how computations are carried out) \citep{belinkov2019analysis,danilevsky2020survey}. 
This thesis focuses on representation-level interpretability, asking whether pretrained RNA representations encode structural information. We use probing to carry out this analysis, and its formulation and methodological considerations are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:bg_probing_nlp}.

\subsection{Probing as a representation-level method}
\label{sec:bg_probing_nlp}
% Probing tests whether a target property is recoverable from pretrained representations by training a restricted auxiliary classifier on top of frozen embeddings \citep{hewitt2019structural,hewitt2019designing}. If a lightweight probe can successfully predict the property of interest, the corresponding information must already be encoded in the representations. 
Probing is a widely used technique for assessing what information is recoverable from learned representations \citep{conneau2018probing,hewitt2019structural}. A restricted auxiliary classifier, the probe, is trained on top of pretrained embeddings that are held fixed (i.e.\ the encoder weights are not updated during probe training), and is asked to predict target property. If a simple probe achieves reasonable accuracy, this is taken as evidence that the relevant information is encoded in the representations. The probe itself is unlikely to reconstruct such information unless it is already accessible in the input embeddings \citep{hewitt2019designing,belinkov2022probing}.

% A central methodological
% concern is probe capacity. If the probe is too expressive, high
% accuracy may reflect the probe's own learning ability rather than
% information present in the representation
% \citep{hewitt2019designing,belinkov2022probing}. Probing is therefore
% most informative when the auxiliary model is deliberately constrained,
% so that successful recovery places the explanatory burden on the
% representation rather than on the probe.
The framework was developed and formalised primarily in NLP, where probing classifiers have been applied to study a wide range of linguistic properties in sentence and word embeddings \citep{conneau2018probing,hewitt2019designing,belinkov2022probing}. A central methodological concern, emphasised throughout this literature, is probe capacity. If the probe is too expressive, high accuracy may reflect the
probe's own modelling power rather than information present in the
representation. Probing is therefore most informative when the auxiliary
model is deliberately constrained, since successful recovery can then be attributed to the representation rather than to the probe itself \citep{hewitt2019designing}.

To capture this structure while controlling probe capacity, we employ a low-rank bilinear probe that projects pretrained nucleotide embeddings into a low-dimensional latent space and scores candidate pairs via a symmetric inner product. The rank parameter functions as an explicit capacity bottleneck, encouraging performance gains to reflect pairwise information already encoded in the embeddings.

The rank parameter controls the dimensionality of this projection, allowing us to test whether the recovered signal is concentrated in a compact subspace.

\subsection{Interpretability in Biological Foundation Models}
\label{sec:bg_interp_protein}

As biological foundation models are increasingly used as reusable
representation backbones, understanding what information their pretrained
representations encode has become an important question
\citep{guo2025foundation,budhkar2025demystifying}. 
This matters most when model-derived signals feed into biological interpretation or serve as priors for downstream analysis.
% This issue is
% particularly important when model-derived signals are used to support
% biological interpretation or to provide priors for downstream analysis.
In such settings, strong predictive performance alone does not establish
what the representations contain, and treating the model as a black box
can limit both interpretability and downstream trustworthiness
\citep{budhkar2025demystifying,zhou2025pathway}.

\textbf{Protein language models.} The most extensive representation-level interpretability work in computational biology has been conducted on protein language models. Attention-based analyses have shown that specific attention heads in
pretrained protein transformers capture structural and functional
properties \citep{rao2021unsupervised,vig2021bertology}. At the representation level, linear classifiers trained on fixed pretrained embeddings have been shown to recover structural properties without any
task-specific supervision \citep{rives2021biological}. Although described as linear projections rather than probes, this is methodologically similar to the probing framework \citep{conneau2018probing,belinkov2022probing}, as both apply a learned
projection to fixed pretrained embeddings to test whether structural
information is recoverable from the representation. More recent mechanistic approaches have examined protein representations through feature decomposition, with sparse autoencoders identifying interpretable latent features aligned with structural motifs and functional domains \citep{simon2025interplm}.

\section{Research gaps and rationale for this study}
\label{sec:bg_gaps}

The review above points to three open questions that this thesis sets out to address.

\textbf{Gap 1: Representational content of RNA FMs.}
FM-backed prediction pipelines achieve strong accuracy on RNA secondary structure benchmarks \citep{wang2025depfold,chen2022rnafm,yin2025ernierna}, but as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:bg_fm_backed}, that accuracy reflects the combined contributions of the pretrained encoder, the prediction head, and the decoder. Whether the pretrained representations themselves already contain base-pairing information, or whether that information is introduced primarily during supervised fine-tuning or decoding, remains an open question. The attention-based analyses reviewed in Section~\ref{sec:bg_interp_protein} provide partial evidence, but attention patterns and learned representations are different objects, and structural signals visible in one need not be recoverable from the other \citep{jain2019attention}.

\textbf{Gap 2: Absence of controlled probing for RNA encoders.}

It has been used extensively in NLP and, more recently, for protein language models~\citep{rao2021unsupervised}, but nobody has applied it systematically to RNA foundation models. As a result, we still do not know whether base-pairing signals are recoverable from pretrained RNA embeddings, in which layers they might be concentrated, or how any of this varies across models of different scale and architectural design.
The probing method \citep{hewitt2019structural} offers a principled method for testing what pretrained representations encode by training a capacity-controlled classifier on embeddings that are held fixed during probe training. It has been applied extensively in NLP \citep{conneau2018probing,belinkov2022probing} and to protein language models, where probing and linear projections have revealed recoverable structural information \citep{rives2021biological,rao2021unsupervised}. As discussed above, some initial RNA work exists, but it is either limited to a single non-encoder-only model \citep{celaj2023atom1} or uses a high-capacity prediction head that cannot isolate what the representations themselves contain \citep{zablocki2025benchmark}. No prior work has applied capacity-controlled pairwise probing systematically across encoder-only RNA foundation models of different scale and design to assess where base-pairing signal is concentrated and how recoverability varies across encoders.

\textbf{Gap 3: Untested downstream value of recovered signals.}
Even if probing demonstrates that base-pairing information is present in
pretrained representations, it does not follow that this information is
practically useful. Probing is a diagnostic tool that measures
recoverability under a particular classifier, not downstream utility. No
prior work has tested whether probe-derived pairwise scores can serve as
soft priors within a thermodynamic or learned folding backend
(Section~\ref{sec:bg_thermo}), or whether such integration remains
effective on RNA families not seen during probe training.

\textbf{Rationale.}
These gaps motivate a two-stage investigation. First, there is a practical reason to care about representational content. If pretrained embeddings already encode base-pairing information, that signal could be reused across prediction pipelines as a lightweight structural prior, reducing reliance on task-specific fine-tuning. This matters increasingly as RNA FMs are adopted in applied settings such as mRNA design and functional annotation, where the reliability of downstream conclusions depends in part on the quality of the underlying representations. Second, there is a model-design motivation. In the protein domain, structural information has been shown to emerge from sequence-only pretraining \citep{rives2021biological,lin2023evolutionary}, but whether an analogous phenomenon occurs in encoder-only RNA language models, and under what architectural or training conditions, remains poorly understood. Controlled evidence on this point can inform future RNA FM development. Third, most probing studies in NLP stop at asking whether a property is encoded, without testing whether the recovered signal has practical downstream value \citep{belinkov2022probing}. Evaluating both recoverability and utility together gives a more complete picture than either analysis alone. The concrete approach is presented in the following chapters.

\section{Problem formulation and scope}
\label{sec:methods_problem}
Let $x=(x_1,\dots,x_L)$ be an RNA sequence of length $L$, where each nucleotide $x_i \in \{\mathrm{A,C,G,U}\}$. We represent the
reference secondary structure as a set of annotated base pairs,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{true}} \subseteq \{(i,j)\mid 1 \le i < j \le L\},
\end{equation}
where \((i,j) \in \mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{true}}\) denotes that nucleotides
at positions \(i\) and \(j\) form a base pair in the reference structure.
Our objective is to predict a base-pair set
\(\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}}\) that satisfies the following
structural constraints.

% \textbf{Local constraints.} As introduced in
% Section~\ref{sec:bg_rna_fundamentals}, local constraints restrict which
% nucleotide pair types are admissible. In this study, we consider three local candidate spaces: \textbf{unconstrained} (all pairs allowed),
% \textbf{Watson-Crick-only} (AU/UA and GC/CG), and \textbf{Watson-Crick+GU}
% (additionally permitting GU/UG wobble pairs). Evaluating all three candidate spaces serves a diagnostic purpose.
% By comparing unconstrained decoding against chemically restricted
% alternatives, we can distinguish the structural signal that the
% representation provides on its own from the benefit of removing
% chemically implausible pairs at decode time. A model whose high scores
% already fall on canonical pair types will see little change when
% candidate restrictions are applied, whereas a model that assigns high
% scores indiscriminately will benefit substantially from the
% restriction. This comparison therefore helps isolate the contribution
% of the representation from that of the decoding interface.
\textbf{Local constraints.} As introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:bg_rna_fundamentals}, local constraints restrict which
nucleotide pair types are admissible during decoding. In RNA,
the canonical Watson-Crick pairs (A-U and G-C) are the most thermodynamically stable and account for most observed base pairs, while G-U wobble pairs are non-canonical but still naturally occurring \citep{varani2000wobble,turner2009nndb}. \textbf{Unconstrained} allows all nucleotide pair types. \textbf{Canonical-only} restricts candidates to Watson-Crick pairs (AU, UA and GC, CG). \textbf{Canonical+wobble} additionally permits GU and UG. Treating canonical-only and canonical+wobble as separate conditions allows us to isolate the effect of permitting wobble pairs, rather than folding all chemically plausible pairs into a single constrained setting. More broadly, comparing all three settings helps distinguish pair recovery that is supported by the score matrix itself from gains that come from the decoder excluding chemically implausible candidates.

\textbf{Global constraints.} We impose only the max-one pairing
constraint (Section~\ref{sec:bg_rna_fundamentals}), requiring that each
nucleotide participates in at most one predicted base pair. We do not
enforce pseudoknot-freeness. Pseudoknots are naturally occurring
structural motifs present in a substantial fraction of known RNA
structures, and several recent methods similarly omit the non-crossing
constraint by treating structure prediction as pairwise classification
\citep{singh2019spotrna,fu2022ufold,wang2026cplfold}. More importantly for our
purposes, keeping the decoder minimal ensures that performance
differences across models can be attributed to the representations
rather than to structural inductive bias in the decoding algorithm.

% Accordingly, $\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}}$ is constructed from a constrained candidate space rather than from the unconstrained set of all nucleotide pairs.
% This pairwise contact-prediction formulation matches the basic object of RNA secondary-structure prediction, namely a set of base pairs, while separating two questions central to this thesis:
% (i) whether base-pair information is accessible from frozen representations and recoverable by a simple probe, and
% (ii) whether the recovered pairwise signal remains useful when incorporated into CPLfold as a downstream structured folding backend.

Accordingly, $\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}}$ is constructed from the set of all nucleotide pairs subject to the local and global constraints defined above, reducing RNA secondary structure prediction to pairwise contact prediction under explicit constraints. Within this formulation, the thesis addresses two questions in separate stages. The first asks whether base-pairing information is recoverable from pretrained representations (Sections~\ref{sec:methods_embeddings}-\ref{sec:methods_decoding_probeonly}). The second asks whether the recovered signal improves prediction when integrated into an existing structured folding backend (Sections~\ref{sec:methods_cplfold}).

\section{Representation extraction}
\label{sec:methods_embeddings}

For each encoder \(m\) and layer \(\ell\), we extract per-nucleotide
representations by passing an input RNA sequence through the
pretrained model,
\begin{equation}
f_{m,\ell}: x \mapsto \mathbf{H}^{(m,\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{L' \times D},
\end{equation}
where \(D\) is the hidden dimension and \(L'\) is the sequence length
after removing non-nucleotide special tokens. We write the
representation at position \(i\) as \(\mathbf{h}_i\in\mathbb{R}^D\), hence

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{H}^{(m,\ell)} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{h}_1^\top\\
\vdots\\
\mathbf{h}_{L'}^\top
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}

Throughout this study, encoder weights are held fixed, and only the probe parameters described in Section~\ref{sec:methods_probe} are updated during training. Keeping the encoder fixed is a deliberate design
choice that follows the standard probing paradigm introduced in earlier work~\citep{alain2017understanding} and formalised in subsequent studies~\citep{hewitt2019structural,belinkov2022probing}. Any
base-pairing signal recovered by the probe can therefore be attributed
to information already present in the pretrained representations. Since the encoder receives no gradient updates during probe training, the result cannot be explained by task-specific fine-tuning. Holding the encoder fixed also ensures a fair
comparison across models and layers, as every encoder is evaluated
through an identical downstream evaluation setup.
% This design is central to the interpretation of the results because the encoder undergoes no task-specific adaptation. Any base-pairing signal recovered by the probe can therefore be attributed to information available in the pretrained representations rather than to end-to-end fine-tuning of the encoder. Keeping the encoder fixed also supports fair comparison across models and layers, since each encoder is evaluated through the same downstream interface.

\section{StructuralContactProbe: a low-rank pairwise probe}
\label{sec:methods_probe}

To test whether base-pairing information is recoverable from these representations, we design a lightweight pairwise probe referred to as the \textbf{StructuralContactProbe}. The probe is intentionally low-capacity, expressive enough to read out pairwise compatibility from the embeddings but not so expressive that it could learn complex structural patterns independently of the encoder. If the probe is too expressive, it may learn the task independently of the representation, making it impossible to determine what the encoder itself contributes~\citep{pimentel2020pareto, hewitt2019control,belinkov2022probing}.

% representations extracted above, we design a lightweight pairwise

% probe is intentionally low-capacity. It should be expressive enough to
% read out pairwise compatibility from the embeddings, but not so
% expressive that it can learn complex structural patterns on its own.

The design builds on the structural probe of Hewitt and Manning~\citep{hewitt2019structural}, which learns a low-rank linear map to test whether syntactic tree distances are reflected in representation geometry. We retain the low-rank projection but replace distance regression with a symmetric bilinear contact score, described in Section~\ref{sec:methods_probe_scoring}. The target property here is not a continuous distance between tokens but a binary label indicating whether two nucleotide positions form a base pair.

\subsection{Projection and scoring}
\label{sec:methods_probe_scoring}
For each position $i$, let $\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{D}$ denote
the embedding extracted in Section~\ref{sec:methods_embeddings}. The
probe learns a single matrix $\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times k}$ that projects each
embedding into a $k$-dimensional subspace. The projected representation is
% \begin{equation}
% \mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{B}^{\top}\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{k}.
% \end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{B}^{\top}\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{k},
\qquad
\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times k}.
\end{equation}
For each candidate pair $(i,j)$ with $i<j$, the probe assigns a symmetric compatibility logit
\begin{equation}
s_{ij} = \mathbf{z}_i^\top \mathbf{z}_j
       = \mathbf{h}_i^\top (\mathbf{B}\mathbf{B}^\top)\mathbf{h}_j,
\end{equation}
and converts logits to probabilities via a sigmoid,
\begin{equation}
p_{ij} = \sigma(s_{ij}) = \frac{1}{1+\exp(-s_{ij})}.
\end{equation}
Base pairing is inherently a pairwise property, and the bilinear formulation reflects this by computing each score from exactly two nucleotide representations. The rank parameter $k$ provides an explicit capacity control. By constraining the probe to a low-dimensional latent subspace, we test whether base-pair compatibility is recoverable through a simple low-rank interaction rather than a highly expressive classifier~\citep{hewitt2019control,pimentel2020pareto}.

\subsection{Pairwise labels}
\label{sec:methods_training_labels}

For each valid candidate pair $(i,j)$, we define a binary label
\begin{equation}
y_{ij} = \mathbb{I}\big[(i,j)\in\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{true}}\big],
\qquad 1 \le i < j \le L'.
\end{equation}
The probe is therefore trained as a binary classifier over nucleotide pairs.

\section{From probe scores to predicted structures}
\label{sec:methods_decoding}
After training, the probe assigns a probability $p_{ij} = \sigma(s_{ij})$ (Section~\ref{sec:methods_probe_scoring}) to every candidate nucleotide pair $(i,j)$ in a given sequence, producing a dense pairwise score matrix.
Converting these continuous scores into
discrete base-pair predictions requires a decoding step. We consider
two settings, corresponding to the two stages of analysis introduced
in Section~\ref{sec:methods_problem}. In the probe-only setting (Section~\ref{sec:methods_decoding_probeonly}), probe scores are used directly to construct a predicted set of base pairs. This provides a direct measure of what can be recovered from the representations alone. In the hybrid folding setting
(Section~\ref{sec:methods_cplfold}), probe scores are passed as soft
priors to CPLfold, testing whether the same signal improves prediction
when combined with a globally constrained folding algorithm.


\subsection{Probe-only decoding}
\label{sec:methods_decoding_probeonly}
Before decoding, admissible nucleotide pairs are restricted according to the evaluation setting, using one of the three pairing constraints defined in Section~\ref{sec:methods_problem}: \textbf{unconstrained}, \textbf{canonical-only}, and \textbf{canonical+wobble}.

% Before decoding, we optionally restrict the candidate pair set
% according to nucleotide identity, using the three candidate spaces
% defined in Section~\ref{sec:methods_problem}: unconstrained,
% Watson-Crick-only, and Watson-Crick+GU.

% Let $\widetilde{p}_{ij}$ denote the score matrix after applying
% upper-triangular masking ($i<j$), an optional pair-type mask, and
% endpoint masking induced by previously selected pairs. We decode
% greedily using a stopping threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{decode}}$:
Decoding operates on a modified score matrix $\widetilde{p}_{ij}$,
obtained from $p_{ij}$ by masking out three categories of entries.
First, only upper-triangular entries ($i < j$) are retained to avoid
duplicate pairs. Second, pairs that violate the chosen pairing
constraint are set to zero. Third, once a pair is selected, all
entries sharing an endpoint with it are removed. Given this matrix,
decoding proceeds greedily with a stopping threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{decode}}$. Here, $\tau$ denotes the minimum predicted probability required for a candidate pair to be accepted.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Select the highest-scoring remaining candidate
          $(i^\ast,j^\ast)=\arg\max_{i<j}\widetilde{p}_{ij}$.
    \item If no valid candidate remains or
          $\widetilde{p}_{i^\ast j^\ast}\le \tau_{\mathrm{decode}}$,
          stop.
    \item Add $(i^\ast,j^\ast)$ to $\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}}$ and
          mask all remaining pairs incident to $i^\ast$ or $j^\ast$.
\end{enumerate}
This decoder enforces only the max-one pairing constraint. In the
illustrative example in Figure~\ref{fig:maxone_decoding}, we set
$\tau_{\mathrm{decode}} = 0.5$. At each step the highest-scoring
pair above the threshold is selected and all pairs sharing an endpoint
are masked. The procedure terminates once no remaining pair exceeds
the threshold.

\subsection{Hybrid folding with CPLfold}
\label{sec:methods_cplfold}

The second setting asks a stronger question: whether probe-derived
pairwise scores improve final secondary-structure prediction when
incorporated into an existing structured folding backend. In this
thesis, \textbf{utility} refers specifically to improvement over
folding without probe-derived priors.

\subsubsection{Exporting probe scores as bonuses}
\label{sec:methods_bonus_export}
The trained probe produces pairwise probabilities \(p_{ij}\) for every
candidate pair in a given sequence
(Section~\ref{sec:methods_probe_scoring}). Concretely, the probe matrix
\(\mathbf{B}\) projects each nucleotide embedding into a
low-dimensional space, pairwise logits are computed as
\(s_{ij} = \mathbf{z}_i^\top \mathbf{z}_j\), and probabilities are
obtained via \(p_{ij} = \sigma(s_{ij})\). We convert this dense output
into a sparse bonus matrix
\(\mathbf{B}^{\mathrm{bonus}} \in \mathbb{R}^{L' \times L'}\) by
applying an export threshold \(\tau_{\mathrm{export}}\):
\begin{equation}
B^{\mathrm{bonus}}_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
p_{ij} & \text{if } p_{ij} \ge \tau_{\mathrm{export}},\\
0      & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Here, a \textbf{bonus} is an additional score attached to a candidate
pair that rewards a structured RNA folding algorithm (hereafter the
\textbf{folding backend}) for including that pair in the predicted
structure. A higher bonus makes the pair more attractive but does not
guarantee its inclusion, following the soft-constraint framework
of~\citep{washietl2012softconstraints}.

Only pairs whose probe scores exceed $\tau_{\mathrm{export}}$ are
passed to the backend. This thresholding reflects the extreme sparsity
of the prediction target. True base pairs account for roughly 0.2\%
of all candidate pairs (Section~\ref{sec:exp_data_pairspace}), so the
raw score matrix is dominated by non-interacting positions. Passing all scores directly to the decoder would dilute the signal from high-confidence candidate pairs with noise from the far more numerous non-pairs. No further structural
filtering is applied at export time. The backend itself decides which
of the retained pairs can coexist in a valid secondary structure. The
specific backends and the mechanism by which bonuses enter the folding
objective are described in
Sections~\ref{sec:methods_backends}--\ref{sec:methods_aug_obj}.

\subsubsection{Folding backends}
\label{sec:methods_backends}

We evaluate two folding backends available in the CPLfold
framework~\citep{wang2026cplfold}. \textbf{ViennaRNA} predicts
pseudoknot-free structures by minimising a free-energy function based
on experimentally measured thermodynamic
parameters~\citep{lorenz2011viennarna,turner2009nndb}.
\textbf{CONTRAfold} replaces these fixed parameters with a scoring
function trained from data, while using the same class of dynamic
programming algorithms~\citep{do2006contrafold}. Testing both backends
lets us check whether probe-derived bonuses yield consistent
improvements under a physics-based and a data-driven scoring model.

\subsubsection{Augmented objective}
\label{sec:methods_aug_obj}
Let $S$ be a candidate structure admissible under the backend constraints, that is, the structural constraints enforced internally by ViennaRNA or CONTRAfold, and let $E_{\mathrm{backend}}(S)$ denote the score assigned to $S$ by the chosen backend. Using the bonus matrix
$\mathbf{B}^{\mathrm{bonus}}$ defined in
Section~\ref{sec:methods_bonus_export}, CPLfold optimises
\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{aug}}(S)
=
E_{\mathrm{backend}}(S)
-
\alpha \sum_{(i,j)\in S} B^{\mathrm{bonus}}_{ij},
\label{eq:aug_obj}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \ge 0$ controls the strength of the probe-derived prior.
When $\alpha = 0$, the method reduces to the unmodified backend. As
$\alpha$ increases, structures containing probe-supported pairs are
increasingly favoured, subject to the admissibility constraints imposed
by the backend.

The trade-off weight $\alpha$ is selected exclusively on the validation
split and held fixed for all test evaluations, including
out-of-distribution settings. Because ViennaRNA and CONTRAfold operate
on different score scales, the effective meaning of a given $\alpha$
is backend-dependent; the search grid and selection protocol are
specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments}.

\section{Encoder models and baselines}
\label{sec:exp_models}

We compare four RNA-pretrained encoder models, RNABERT \citep{akiyama2022rnabert}, RNA-FM \citep{chen2022rnafm}, ERNIE-RNA \citep{yin2025ernierna}, and RiNALMo \citep{penic2025rinalmo}, together with RoBERTa \citep{liu2019roberta} as a general-purpose masked-language-model baseline. Architectural and pretraining details for each model are reviewed in Chapter~\ref{ch:background}. For each encoder, layer-wise representations
$\mathbf{H}^{(m,\ell)}$ are extracted without updating the
encoder weights and passed to the common probe and decoding
pipeline described in Chapter~\ref{ch:methods}.

As a non-contextual baseline, we also include a one-hot nucleotide representation. Each nucleotide is mapped to a fixed four-dimensional vector,

\section{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:methods_metrics}

The goal of the probing experiments is to evaluate whether representations produced by pretrained encoders, without any task-specific training of the
encoder weights, support recovery of correct base-pair indices. This requires a metric that measures exact positional match between predicted and reference pairs, rather than broader measures of structural similarity such as tree-edit distance or mountain distance.

\subsection{Pair-level precision, recall, and F1}
\label{sec:methods_metrics_f1}

We adopt pair-level precision, recall, and F1, which are standard in
RNA secondary structure benchmarking \citep{mathews2019benchmark} and
directly measure exact contact recovery. Predicted and reference pair
sets are compared under exact index matching:
$\mathrm{TP}=|\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}} \cap \mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{true}}|$,
$\mathrm{FP}=|\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}} \setminus \mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{true}}|$,
$\mathrm{FN}=|\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{true}} \setminus \mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{pred}}|$.
% From these, precision, recall, and F1 are computed as
% \begin{align}
% \mathrm{Precision} &= \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}},\\
% \mathrm{Recall} &= \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN}},\\
% \mathrm{F1} &= \frac{2\,\mathrm{Precision}\,\mathrm{Recall}}
% {\mathrm{Precision}+\mathrm{Recall}}.
% \end{align}
From these, precision, recall, and F1 are computed as
\[
\mathrm{Precision}=\frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}}, \quad
\mathrm{Recall}=\frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN}}, \quad
\mathrm{F1}=\frac{2 \times \mathrm{Precision} \times \mathrm{Recall}}{\mathrm{Precision}+\mathrm{Recall}}.
\]

F1 serves as the primary selection criterion throughout because it
balances over-prediction against under-prediction, which is
particularly important given the extreme class imbalance in the
pair space (Section~\ref{sec:exp_data_pairspace}).
% F1 is used as the primary selection criterion throughout this study
% because it balances over-prediction (low precision) against
% under-prediction (low recall), which is particularly important given
% the extreme class imbalance in the pair space
% (Section~\ref{sec:exp_data_pairspace}). 
It is used for probe configuration selection on VL0
(Section~\ref{sec:exp_selection_criterion}), for CPLfold $\alpha$
selection (Section~\ref{sec:exp_alpha}), and as the main reported
metric in all held-out evaluations in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\subsection{Canonical pairing rate}
\label{sec:methods_metrics_canon}
In addition to F1, we report the canonical pairing rate as a
diagnostic measure of whether predicted pairs are chemically plausible.
Given a predicted pair set $\mathcal{S}$, this is defined as the
fraction of pairs whose nucleotide types belong to a canonical
base-pair class $\mathcal{C}$:
\[
\mathrm{CanonRate}
= \frac{|\{(i,j)\in\mathcal{S} \mid (x_i,x_j)\in\mathcal{C}\}|}
       {|\mathcal{S}|}.
\]
We consider two canonical classes:
$\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{WC{+}GU}}=\{AU,UA,GC,CG,GU,UG\}$ and
$\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{WC}}=\{AU,UA,GC,CG\}$. Unlike F1, which
measures positional correctness against a reference structure, the
canonical pairing rate evaluates whether a model's predictions fall on
chemically plausible pair types regardless of whether the predicted
positions are correct. This metric is applied to predicted pair sets
under unconstrained decoding in
Section~\ref{sec:results_canonical_rate_unconstrained}.

\section{Evaluation settings}
\label{sec:exp_conditions}
We evaluate each encoder under two settings, designed to address the two central questions of the thesis: whether base-pairing information is recoverable from
pretrained representations, and whether the recovered signal improves
structured RNA folding.

\textbf{Probe-only decoding.}
Probe-derived probabilities are decoded using greedy max-one
decoding, without invoking any structured folding algorithm. This
evaluation tests whether base-pairing information is recoverable
from pretrained representations alone.

\textbf{Hybrid CPLfold-based folding.}
In the second setting, probe scores are integrated into CPLfold. We
evaluate two variants within this setting:

\emph{Thermodynamic-only folding.}
CPLfold is run with $\alpha = 0$, so that no probe-derived evidence is
injected. This serves as the structured-folding baseline and isolates
the performance of the backend decoder alone.

\emph{Hybrid folding.}
Probe-derived pairwise scores are exported as bonuses and passed to
CPLfold with $\alpha > 0$. This condition tests whether
representation-derived evidence provides additional utility when
combined with a globally constrained folding objective.

\section{CPLfold validation and $\alpha$ selection}
\label{sec:exp_alpha}

For each representation source, we first fix the
validation-selected probe configuration
$(\ell^\ast, k^\ast, \mathrm{mode}^\ast, \tau^\ast)$.
Using this fixed configuration, we export a bonus file for each RNA sequence
in VL0, TS0, and NEW, containing probe-derived base-pair scores
for all candidate pairs (Section~\ref{sec:methods_bonus_export}). These bonus files are then supplied to CPLfold under both the ViennaRNA and CONTRAfold backends, where $\alpha$ controls the relative weight of
probe-derived scores in the folding objective.

For each model-backend pair, we sweep the trade-off weight
\begin{equation}
\alpha \in \{0.00, 0.02, 0.04, \dots, 2.00\}.
\end{equation}
The optimal $\alpha$ is selected on VL0 by maximising the mean per-sequence F1:
\begin{equation}
\alpha^\ast
=
\arg\max_{\alpha}
\frac{1}{|\mathrm{VL0}|}
\sum_{x \in \mathrm{VL0}} \mathrm{F1}(x;\alpha).
\end{equation}
% This criterion is appropriate for the downstream folding setting,
% where the goal is to produce one structure per sequence and
% sequence-level structural quality is the primary quantity of interest. 
% Once selected for a given model--backend pair, $\alpha^\ast$ is held
% fixed for evaluation on TS0 and NEW.
% No further tuning is performed on held-out data.
We select $\alpha$ using per-sequence F1 because RNA folding
produces a single predicted structure for each sequence, making
the individual sequence the appropriate unit of evaluation.

comparison model, yielding a paired difference per sequence. We then test whether the mean paired difference differs significantly

sequence level.

\section{Interpretation of main findings}
This study examines whether pretrained RNA encoders encode base-pairing information in their representations and, if so, whether that information is useful for practical structure prediction. Three main findings emerge from the study. 

First, the amount of base-pairing signal recoverable by a probe varies dramatically across encoders. ERNIE-RNA is far ahead of all others, and RoBERTa outperforms several RNA-specific models despite having no RNA-related pretraining. For the strongest encoders, a projection rank of just $k{=}32$ suffices, and increasing rank beyond this point degrades rather than improves performance. This pattern is consistent with prior findings that syntactic tree structure in BERT is recoverable through low-rank probes \citep{hewitt2019structural}, and suggests that the base-pairing information accessible to our probe likewise occupies a compact subspace in the encoder's representation.

Second, measured performance depends not only on the encoder but also on the evaluation setup. Allowed pairing constraints, threshold calibration, and layer selection all have large, model-specific effects on measured F1.

Third, probe-derived scores can improve structured folding when injected as soft priors into CPLfold, but the gains depend on the folding backend. CONTRAfold benefits consistently across all tested encoders, while ViennaRNA improves mainly with ERNIE-RNA and can degrade on OOD data for weaker priors.

\subsection{Interpretation of ERNIE-RNA's performance}
\label{sec:disc_ernie}

ERNIE-RNA performs substantially better than every other encoder across all evaluations in this study. One plausible explanation is its architectural bias toward canonical base pairing. Unlike the other models tested, ERNIE-RNA modifies the transformer's attention mechanism so that canonical pairs (GC, AU, and GU) receive fixed bonuses in the first attention layer, and this information is propagated through subsequent layers via attention-map initialisation~\citep{yin2025ernierna}. This does not demonstrate that the model has learned RNA structure in a broader sense, but it may make canonical pairing information more directly available in the resulting representations. The unconstrained decoding results support this reading. ERNIE-RNA achieves a canonical pairing rate above 71\% (Table~\ref{tab:canonical_ts_new}), well above the dataset reference level of roughly 38\%, whereas the other encoders remain much closer to that baseline.

A second factor is the possibility of data leakage. ERNIE-RNA was pretrained on roughly 20 million RNAcentral sequences~\citep{yin2025ernierna}, and our held-out sets were drawn from overlapping public repositories~\citep{danaee2018bprna, kalvari2017rfam}, so some held-out sequences may have been seen during pretraining. However, RNA-FM and RNABERT were also pretrained on RNAcentral-derived corpora yet perform substantially worse, suggesting that data overlap alone does not explain the full gap. The absolute performance performance of ERNIE-RNA should therefore be interpreted with caution, while the relative comparison across models under a common protocol remains informative.

\subsection{RoBERTa and the effect of allowed pairing constraints}
\label{sec:disc_roberta}
Under unconstrained decoding, RoBERTa, a general-purpose language model, performs comparably to or slightly below RNA-FM on TS0. Once the allowed pairs are restricted to canonical types, however, it rises to second place on both TS0 and NEW, ahead of every RNA-pretrained encoder except ERNIE-RNA. The canonical pairing-rate diagnostic helps explain this. RoBERTa's unconstrained predictions show no preference for canonical pairs, remaining close to the reference level expected from nucleotide composition alone. Despite this, the probe still recovers a non-trivial base-pairing signal under unconstrained decoding, suggesting that the representations carry some position-level information relevant to pairing, even though they lack nucleotide-type specificity. Why a general-purpose English language model produces any useful signal for RNA base pairing is not clear from our experiments. One possibility is that learning long-range dependencies during pretraining shapes the transformer's representational geometry in a way that incidentally supports pairwise scoring, even on a domain as different as RNA. Restricting the allowed pairs provides the missing chemical prior, filtering out implausible candidates and letting this position-level signal operate only over valid pair types.

The size of the constraint effect varies predictably across models. For ERNIE-RNA, whose unconstrained predictions already concentrate on canonical pairs (above 71\%), filtering yields only a moderate gain. For RoBERTa, the same restriction produces a much larger relative improvement (+82.7\%) by eliminating a large fraction of false positives. RNA-FM selects the unconstrained setting as optimal on VL0 and shows no benefit from filtering at all (Table~\ref{tab:best_config_val}). These differences indicate that the effect of allowed pairing constraints depends on how many non-canonical predictions a model produces without them. Models that already concentrate on canonical pairs gain little from filtering, while models that do not, such as RoBERTa, benefit substantially. As a result, the choice of constraint can change which models appear stronger under the same evaluation.


\subsection{Generalisation across RNA families}
\label{sec:disc_generalisation}
Cross-family generalisation is a known challenge in RNA structure prediction. Prior work has shown that models trained or evaluated within the same RNA families often fail to maintain performance when tested on unseen families~\citep{szikszai2022}. Our results are broadly consistent with this pattern, but the behaviour is more varied than a uniform drop. Under each model's validation-selected best configuration, RNA-FM drops by 25.9\% from TS0 to NEW (Table~\ref{tab:probe_best_full}), and ERNIE-RNA also degrades moderately. RoBERTa, however, shows no degradation and in fact improves slightly on NEW. One possible explanation is that RNA-specific encoders may overfit to family-level regularities seen during pretraining, whereas RoBERTa, having never seen RNA sequences, has no such bias and may therefore generalise more evenly across families.

A similar pattern appears in the CPLfold experiments. As the CPLfold baseline ($\alpha = 0$) already produces higher F1 on NEW than on TS0 under both backends, we compare the absolute improvement each probe prior adds over each split's own baseline. Under CONTRAfold, ERNIE-RNA's prior adds a similar absolute gain on both splits. RNA-FM's gain drops sharply from TS0 to NEW, while RoBERTa gains slightly more on NEW. Under ViennaRNA, the contrast becomes clearer. RNA-FM's VL0-selected $\alpha$ actually degrades performance on NEW (Table~\ref{tab:alpha_vienna}), showing that a configuration optimised on in-distribution data does not necessarily transfer to unseen families. Thus, the probe-only and CPLfold results show a consistent pattern. RNA-FM is the most OOD-sensitive, ERNIE-RNA is moderately robust, and RoBERTa is the most stable. 

The length-stratified analysis adds a further observation. Degradation on NEW is most pronounced for longer sequences, suggesting that generalisation is limited not only by the diversity of training families but also by sequence length.

Under canonical pairing constraints, ERNIE-RNA degrades moderately from TS0 to NEW, RoBERTa remains essentially stable, and RNA-FM drops by 25.9\% relative to TS0 (Table~\ref{tab:probe_best_full}). The length-stratified analysis (Tables~\ref{tab:appendix_f1_by_length_ts0},~\ref{tab:appendix_f1_by_length_new}) further shows that degradation on NEW is most pronounced for longer sequences, suggesting that the probe's ability to generalise to novel families is limited by both training-set diversity and sequence length. In the CPLfold setting, RNA-FM under ViennaRNA illustrates this concretely. Its VL0-selected $\alpha$ actually degrades performance on NEW (Table~\ref{tab:alpha_vienna}), showing that a configuration optimised on in-distribution data may not transfer to unseen families. ERNIE-RNA under CONTRAfold, by contrast, produces consistent gains on both splits, providing stronger evidence of robust utility.

\subsection{Downstream utility and backend dependence}
\label{sec:disc_utility}
The CPLfold experiments test whether recovered base-pairing information is useful in practice, not just recoverable. Under CONTRAfold, probe-derived priors produce consistent improvements for every encoder tested, with ERNIE-RNA benefiting most but RNA-FM and RoBERTa also showing gains on both held-out splits.
This suggests that even a simple linear probe can extract pairwise signals complementary to what a structured folding algorithm captures on its own.
 
Under ViennaRNA, improvements are largely confined to ERNIE-RNA, and RNA-FM's VL0-selected $\alpha$ actually degrades performance on NEW. One plausible explanation for this asymmetry is the difference in how the two backends assign scores. ViennaRNA uses free-energy parameters in physical units (kcal/mol), whereas CONTRAfold uses parameters learned from data without a fixed physical interpretation. Adding a probe-derived probability as a bonus term creates a scale mismatch in both cases. However, when neither the backend scores nor the bonus has a fixed physical units, the $\alpha$ sweep may be better able to compensate for that mismatch. This is one possible explanation for why CONTRAfold accommodates probe priors more broadly, though we have not tested this hypothesis directly.

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}
This study uses linear probing to assess whether secondary-structure information is encoded in pretrained representations. Linear probing has been noted to test what is recoverable under a particular probe family, rather than fully characterising what a representation contains~\citep{hewitt2019structural, chowdhury2024probing}. Complementary interpretability methods could help address this. In the protein domain, for instance, recent work has applied sparse autoencoders to ESM-2~\citep{simon2025interplm, adams2025mechanistic} and used categorical Jacobian analysis to reveal pairwise coevolutionary statistics stored in learned representations~\citep{zhang2024plm}. Applying a broader set of interpretability methods to RNA encoders could help uncover not only whether base-pairing information is recoverable, but also how it is organised within these models.

% On the architecture side, all encoders tested are BERT-based encoder-only transformers, so it remains unclear whether the patterns we observe, such as the dominance of ERNIE-RNA or the competitive performance of RoBERTa, would hold for other architectures. Extending the probing framework to decoder-only models such as GenerRNA~\citep{zhao2024generrna} or encoder-decoder architectures would help answer this. It would also be worth comparing ERNIE-RNA against other structure-aware encoders like RNAErnie~\citep{wang2024rnaernie} or structRFM, which would allow a more controlled test of which forms of architectural prior most effectively make structural information accessible to downstream probes.

All tested encoders are BERT-based encoder-only transformers, so it remains unclear whether the observed patterns would hold for decoder-only models such as GenerRNA~\citep{zhao2024generrna} or encoder-decoder architectures. Comparing ERNIE-RNA with other structure-aware encoders such as RNAErnie~\citep{wang2024rnaernie} and structRFM~\citep{zhu2025structrfm} would allow a more direct comparison of which architectural priors most effectively make structural information accessible..

Our evaluation is drawn almost entirely from Rfam-derived non-coding RNA families~\citep{danaee2018bprna, kalvari2017rfam}, and OOD generalisation relies on a single family-level split. Other key structured RNA classes, including coding RNA local structures and long non-coding RNA domains, are not represented. Testing on extreme-length or low-sequence-identity subsets, or on experimentally determined in vivo structures such as icSHAPE~\citep{spitale2015icshape} would provide a stronger test of robustness. It would also be worth applying the same probing framework to tasks beyond secondary structure, such as RNA-protein interaction or modification site prediction, to see what else these representations encode.

Finally, the extent of overlap between ERNIE-RNA's pretraining corpus and our evaluation sets remains unquantified. We used preprocessed splits from bpRNA~\citep{danaee2018bprna}, but did not perform sequence-level deduplication against ERNIE-RNA's RNAcentral-derived pretraining data~\citep{yin2025ernierna} ourselves. A systematic deduplication analysis, or evaluation on a genuinely new benchmark built from sequences absent from existing pretraining corpora, would help clarify how much of ERNIE-RNA's advantage reflects genuine representational quality and how much may be inflated by data leakage.

Comparing ERNIE-RNA against other structure-aware models like RNAErnie~\citep{wang2024rnaernie} or structRFM would also allow a more controlled test of which forms of architectural prior most effectively make structural information accessible.

% Finally, the extent of overlap between ERNIE-RNA's pretraining corpus and our evaluation sets remains unquantified (Section~\ref{sec:disc_ernie}). We used preprocessed splits from bpRNA~\citep{danaee2018bprna} without sequence-level deduplication against ERNIE-RNA's RNAcentral-derived pretraining data~\citep{yin2025ernierna}. A systematic deduplication analysis would help clarify how much of ERNIE-RNA's advantage reflects genuine representational quality and how much may be inflated by data leakage.

\section{Concluding remarks}
This study examined whether pretrained RNA encoders contain information about RNA base pairing in their representations, even without explicit structural supervision. The results show that the answer depends on the encoder. ERNIE-RNA, which builds base-pairing bias into its attention mechanism, consistently produces representations from which a simple probe can recover substantial structural information. By contrast, most other RNA-focused encoders perform much less well under the same setup.

The results also show that performance depends not only on the encoder but on how the evaluation is set up. RoBERTa, despite having no RNA-specific pretraining, outperforms several RNA-pretrained encoders once allowed pairing constraints are introduced. This shows that the same representation can appear weak or competitive depending on whether the evaluation compensates for what the representation lacks.

The CPLfold experiments further show that probe-derived pairwise scores can improve structured RNA folding, especially with CONTRAfold. These gains are not equally reliable across all backends and settings, but they still suggest that information recovered from pretrained representations can be useful for downstream structure prediction.

Overall, the findings help clarify the extent to which pretrained RNA encoders capture RNA secondary structure. They also suggest that future progress will depend not only on larger models or more pretraining data, but also on model design, careful evaluation, and stronger connections between representation analysis and downstream prediction. This work is intended as a starting point for further study of structural information in pretrained RNA representations and its use in RNA structure prediction.
